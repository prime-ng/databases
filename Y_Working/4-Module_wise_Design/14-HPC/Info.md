2. ### What are the top 5 cognitive skills?
   - Attention, Working Memory, Processing Speed, Executive Functions, and Cognitive Flexibility (or Problem-Solving)
     Here's a breakdown of these key skills:
     - Attention: The ability to focus, filter distractions, and sustain concentration on tasks, essential for absorbing new information. 
     - Working Memory: Your mental workspace for holding and manipulating information short-term (like remembering a phone number while dialing), crucial for reasoning and learning. 
     - Processing Speed: How quickly your brain can take in, understand, and respond to information efficiently. 
     - Executive Functions: The brain's control center, including planning, organizing, decision-making, and self-control, guiding complex tasks. 
     - Cognitive Flexibility/Problem-Solving: The capacity to adapt thinking, see multiple perspectives, and find solutions to challenges, involving logic and abstract thought.

4. ### What are the five cognitive levels?
   There are six levels of cognitive learning according to the revised version of Bloom's Taxonomy. Each level is conceptually different. The six levels are remembering, understanding, applying, analyzing, evaluating, and creating.

   **Here's a breakdown of these cognitive levels:**
   - **Remembering:** The ability to recall information, facts, and concepts, such as definitions, formulas, and procedures.
   - **Understanding:** The ability to interpret, explain, and translate information into different forms, such as converting a formula into a graph or explaining a concept in your own words.
   - **Applying:** The ability to use information in new situations, such as solving a problem or creating a solution.
   - **Analyzing:** The ability to break down information into its component parts, such as identifying the cause and effect of a situation or comparing and contrasting different perspectives.
   - **Evaluating:** The ability to make judgments about the quality and accuracy of information, such as critiquing a solution or comparing different options.
   - **Creating:** The ability to generate new ideas, solutions, or products, such as designing a new product or creating a new process.

6. ### What are the 4 stages of competency?
   The Four Stages of Competence are a learning model that describes the various psychological stages we go through when learning a new skill: Unconscious competence (ignorance), conscious incompetence (awareness), conscious competence (learning) and unconscious competence (mastery).
   **Here's a breakdown of these stages:**
   - **Unconscious competence (ignorance):** The ability to perform a skill without conscious awareness, such as riding a bike or typing.
   - **Conscious incompetence (awareness):** The ability to perform a skill with conscious awareness, such as learning to ride a bike or typing.
   - **Conscious competence (learning):** The ability to perform a skill with conscious awareness, such as learning to ride a bike or typing.
   - **Unconscious competence (mastery):** The ability to perform a skill without conscious awareness, such as riding a bike or typing.

## The Hierarchy of Learning

1. ### The HPC is structured like a tree, moving from broad goals to specific observable actions:
   - Domain: The broad area of development (e.g., Cognitive Development).
   - Curricular Goal: The high-level objective within that domain.
   - Competency: A specific skill or behavior that can be observed.
   - Learning Outcomes (LOs): The granular, measurable milestones that prove a competency is being met.

   **Example:**
   - Domain: Language and Literacy. 
   - Curricular Goal: Effective communication in two languages.
   - Competency: Converses fluently and holds meaningful conversations.
   - Learning Outcome: "Participates in short conversations about a familiar story."

2. ### The 3 Types of Ability (Core Descriptors)
   In the HPC (especially for Middle and Secondary stages), every activity is assessed against three specific "Abilities." These describe how the child approached the task rather than just the final answer.

**Ability** - Awareness
**Definition** - Knowledge of the task and its requirements; being attentive to surroundings and instructions
**Example in Action** - The student understands why a science experiment is being done and knows all the materials needed.

**Ability** - Sensitivity
**Definition** - Managing emotions, showing empathy towards peers, and being attuned to social norms during the task
**Example in Action** - During a group project, the student listens to others' ideas and helps a peer who is struggling.

**Ability** - Creativity
**Definition** - Generating original solutions, thinking "out of the box," and exploring diverse possibilities
**Example in Action** - Instead of a standard poster, the student creates a 3D model using recycled waste to explain a concept.

3. ### Descriptor Levels: The "Stream, Mountain, Sky" Metaphor
To make assessment positive and non-judgmental, NEP 2020 uses metaphors for the three performance levels.

**Beginner (The Stream - $\approx$):**
**Meaning:** The child is just starting their journey. They need significant support from teachers to complete tasks.
**Example:** "Requires help to identify basic shapes in the environment." 

**Proficient (The Mountain - $\triangle$):**
**Meaning:** The child has potential and is climbing toward success. They can do the task but occasionally need a "nudge" or peer support.
**Example:** "Can perform simple addition but needs a number line for support." 

**Advanced (The Sky - $\bigcirc$):**
**Meaning:** The child has achieved mastery. They can perform the task independently or even lead/help others.
**Example:** "Solves complex word problems independently and explains the logic to classmates."

4. ### Competency Types
The HPC tracks progress across five distinct types of competencies to ensure "Holistic" growth:

**Cognitive:** Logical thinking, problem-solving, and literacy (e.g., analyzing a poem).

**Meta-cognitive:** "Thinking about thinking"—the ability to self-reflect (e.g., "I realized I made a mistake in step 2 and corrected it").

**Social & Emotional:** Empathy, ethics, and collaboration (e.g., following classroom rules).

**Physical:** Fine and gross motor skills (e.g., precision in using scissors or balance in yoga).

**Aesthetic/Practical:** Creativity in arts, crafts, and vocational skills (e.g., clay modeling).

5. ### Multi-Source Assessment Categories
Unlike traditional cards where only the teacher writes, the HPC has sections for:

**Self-Assessment:** "I feel proud when I..." or "I want to learn more about..."

**Peer Assessment:** "My friend helped me by..." or "My friend was very creative today."

**Parent Feedback:** Observations of the child's behavior at home (e.g., "Helps in kitchen," "Spends time reading").

**Teacher Observations:** Descriptive anecdotes instead of "Good" or "Keep it up."

### What is the term "Assignment" used in the ecosystem of NEP 2020?

The term "Assignment" is used within the ecosystem of NEP 2020, though its role and definition have been significantly transformed.

While the original 66-page policy document (2020) focuses more on broader terms like "Assessment," the subsequent implementation guidelines—such as the National Curriculum Framework (NCF) 2023 and the National Credit Framework (NCrF)—use "Assignment" in two very specific ways.

1. **The Pedagogical Meaning: "Task-Based Assessment"**
In the classroom context, NEP 2020 replaces the traditional idea of "rote-learning homework" with Formative Assignments.
**What it is:** It is no longer just a set of questions to be copied from a textbook. Under NEP, an "assignment" is defined as a competency-based task designed to test a student's application of knowledge rather than memory.

1. **The Shift:**
  **From:** Repetitive writing (Homework).
  **To:** Creative projects, community-based surveys, or "toy-based" learning tasks.

  **The Holistic Progress Card (HPC):** Assignments now form a core part of the student's Portfolio. Instead of a single final exam score, these "assigned tasks" are tracked throughout the year to show a child's growth in critical thinking and life skills.

2. **The Technical Meaning:** "Assignment of Credits"In Higher Education and the National Credit Framework (NCrF), the term "Assignment" is used in a technical sense.
  **What it is:** It refers to the formal allocation of credits to a student based on their learning hours.
  **The Context:** When you complete a course, internship, or even a vocational project, the "Assignment of Credits" is the process of recording that achievement in your Academic Bank of Credits (ABC).
  **Integration:** This allows for "Multiple Entry and Multiple Exit" points, where credits "assigned" to you for a certificate can later be used toward a full degree.
3. **"Take-Home" vs. "Classroom" Assignments** The NEP implementation guidelines (specifically for the Foundational and Preparatory stages) distinguish between types of assignments:
  **Foundational Stage (Ages 3–8):** There is a "No Homework" policy. "Assignments" here are purely play-based activities done in school.
  **Middle & Secondary Stage:** Assignments become "exploratory" or "discovery-based," such as interviewing elders for a history project or conducting a small science experiment at home.

  | Feature         | Traditional Assignment              | NEP 2020 Assignment                            |
